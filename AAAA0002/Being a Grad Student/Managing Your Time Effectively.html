Week 3 Being a Grad Student

Managing Your Time Effectively

Graduate study requires a thoughtful approach to time management. With multiple responsibilities competing for your attention—work, family, personal time, and academics—developing a sustainable schedule is key to staying on track and avoiding burnout.

Understanding your availability is crucial for online learners. Ask yourself when the best time is for you to take courses, how much time you can realistically dedicate to coursework each week, and how you will complete your coursework in light of your availability and the courses’ deadlines. Graduate courses typically require 15–20 hours per week of study time, so planning ahead ensures you meet deadlines and participate in discussions in a meaningful way.

Using a weekly planner can help you chart your daily activities, including work, commuting, family commitments, meals, hobbies, social time, schoolwork, and sleep. Be sure to block out consistent time for coursework each week and treat these blocks like scheduled classes that are non-negotiable and protected.

Remember that balance is essential. Make sure your schedule includes time for rest, relationships, and personal well-being. Avoid overcommitting by being honest about your capacity.

Set Mini-Deadlines for Big Projects

Break down essays, presentations, or readings into smaller chunks with check-in points along the way. That will enable you to make steady progress.

Scenario:

Nora is an online graduate student working on a 15-page research paper that is due in three weeks. Instead of waiting until the last minute, she breaks the project into smaller tasks with mini-deadlines:

  • Week 1:
    • Days 1–2: Choose topic and get instructor approval.
    • Days 3–5: Conduct initial research and gather sources.
    • Days 6–7: Create outline and thesis statement.
  • Week 2:
    • Days 1–3: Write first draft of introduction and literature review.
    • Days 4–6: Draft methodology and findings sections.
    • Days 7: Check in with peer or mentor for feedback.
  • Week 3:
    • Days 1–2: Revise based on feedback.
    • Days 3–4: Finalize conclusion and references.
    • Day 5: Proofread and format.
    • Days 6–7: Submit paper and reflect on process.

By setting these mini-deadlines, Nora stays on track, reduces stress, and ensures her work is high quality and well paced.

Prioritize Wisely

Focus your energy where it matters most, by sorting tasks based on their urgency, importance, and impact. Here are three effective approaches.

Eisenhower Matrix

Divide tasks into four categories: (1) urgent and important, (2) important but not urgent, (3) urgent but not important, and (4) neither. This helps clarify what you should tackle now versus delegate or postpone. 



To access the transcript, please view the Eisenhower Matrix video on YouTube [webpage].

ABC Method

Rank tasks as A (must do), B (should do), or C (nice to do) to keep high-priority items front and center. 

To access the transcript, please view the ABC Method video on YouTube [webpage].

Impact vs. Effort Grid

Evaluate tasks based on how valuable the outcome is versus how much time/energy it takes. Focus on “high impact, low effort” wins first.
