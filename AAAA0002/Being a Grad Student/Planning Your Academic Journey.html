Week 3 Being a Grad Student

Planning Your Academic Journey

To succeed academically, it is vital to understand your program’s structure and requirements, as well as how to navigate them effectively. This will help you reach your graduation timeline goals. 

Understand Your Degree Program

Be sure to learn all about your program so that you know what to expect and can ensure it is in alignment with your personal and professional goals. Research online and reach out to an advisor if you have questions. Advisors are available via phone, email, or live chat.

Map Your Course Path

As you review your program’s course requirements, take the time to plan

⚠️Important: Reach out to your Advising Team to help with this process. They have course progression knowledge and resources that can assist you. 

Follow these steps to map your course path:

Plan Ahead: Map out the course progression early

Taking the time to map out your course progression early can make a significant difference in your academic journey. With new sessions starting each month at APUS, you have the flexibility to build a schedule that aligns with your goals and personal commitments. Begin by carefully reviewing your program’s requirements and organizing your Core, Concentration, and Elective courses in a logical order (see the next section for more details). 

It is also important to keep financial aid and registration deadlines in mind when planning your courses. Staying aware of academic year nuances and registration dates can help ensure your enrollment proceeds smoothly without unexpected obstacles. 

If you are unsure about the best path forward or have questions about sequencing courses, contact the Advising Team. They can help you create a course map tailored to your needs and keep you on track to meet your graduation goals. First Required Course

Begin your studies by registering for the designated first required course in the program, which is categorized as an Institutional Requirement on your Academic Plan.

The Department Chair chose this course to establish foundational knowledge and prepare students for their graduate program. This valuable class, which is included in most master’s degree plans, is key to developing the research skills and academic habits necessary for future success and smooth progression through subsequent registrations.

Unsure how to register? Review the How to Register guide [webpage] in the Success Center. 

Core vs. Concentration Courses and Integrating Electives

⚠️Note: This section does not apply to Doctoral Students

Doctoral students should reach out to doctoralstudies@apus.edu for assistance with course planning, as these degree plans are not laid out the same way as Master's students' plans. For example , you cannot take electives or other courses in any order, as there are only certain courses running each term.

Master's Students -- Be sure to complete the program’s Core courses in the suggested course progression order (as described earlier) before moving on to the Concentration courses in the program. Core courses set the foundation for the more complex content in the Concentration classes. 

Ideally, Concentration courses will be taken after you have satisfied all of the Core requirements, but before you tackle the final program requirement. These courses allow students to narrow the topic for the capstone thesis or project.

A Tip from Advising

Because the Core requirements are essentially the same across all Concentration options within the same program, we recommend completing these first. 

Unsure of which Concentration to choose? Not to worry! By starting with the Core requirements, you will be less likely to lose credit if you decide to change Concentrations, while still making progress toward degree completion.

If the degree program includes one or more Elective courses for completion, insert them into your schedule throughout your academic journey. The Electives can come from almost any graduate-level subject at APUS, as long as a prerequisite is not required. These courses can be a welcome break from the discipline and give you familiarity with topics you would not otherwise have encountered in your degree.

Exploring your interests? Electives are a great way to do that! Just make sure you take the Electives after your Core courses to help solidify your Concentration selection. 

A Tip from Advising

Simply put, Electives have scheduling flexibility. Some students opt to save them for their last course, just before the final program requirement.

Scheduling Tip: Register Early

Be mindful of course availability during your time of study. Some specialized courses may be offered on a limited schedule to ensure quality classroom experiences. This is why it is important to map out your registrations 6–12 months in advance. Staying proactive and working with the Advising Team to confirm course schedules will help you maintain steady progress toward your degree.

Research also shows that registering early is a key habit of successful students. By securing your seat in advance, you help ensure your payment processes smoothly and give yourself time to prepare for the academic demands ahead.

Use the online Course Schedule [webpage], in conjunction with your Academic Plan, to plan your course schedule.

Scheduling Expectations

Course load requirements change from the undergraduate level to the graduate level. Additionally, expectations for full-time/part-time status vary based on funding type.

If you're using FSA to pay for your education, you must be enrolled in an FSA Academic Year, which includes two back-to-back 16-week semesters.

Key Requirements
  • You must take at least one course in the first month of your first semester.
  • Every course must start and end within the 16-week semester dates.
  • All courses for the semester should be registered by the *census date, even if they begin later.
  • If there are no course registrations by the census date, FSA will not be disbursed for the semester.

*Census date: The date your enrollment is locked for FSA eligibility. It’s the second Monday of each semester.

Enrollment Status
  • Full-time = 6 credit hours per semester
    • One course must start in month 1.
    • It's recommended to start your second course in month 3 to avoid overlap (most APUS courses are 8 weeks long).
  • Part-time = 3 credit hours per semester
    • Your course must begin in month 1 of the semester.

Maintain Momentum

To keep your academic momentum strong, commit to registering for courses consistently and doing so early. Successful students understand that steady progress is not just a habit—it’s a strategy. Staying enrolled and following a routine brings you closer to graduation, helps protect your financial aid, and keeps your academic record strong. Every course you complete adds to your growth, whereas unnecessary withdrawals can leave marks on your transcript and raise concerns with future employers. Approach each term with intention and determination. Building momentum means more than simply moving ahead; it is about developing a mindset of resilience, focus, and purpose that will guide you all the way to your goals.

Remember: When do you want to graduate? 

Be Prepared for the Time Commitment

While you may anticipate a workload similar to your undergraduate studies, keep in mind that graduate-level expectations are substantially higher. Before enrolling, it is important to understand the time commitment required at this academic level.

Master's Students should anticipate dedicating approximately 18–22 hours per week to each course.

Doctoral Students should anticipate dedicating approximately 25–30 hours per week to each course.

Being aware of and planning for this expectation in advance empowers you to stay organized, keep up with coursework, and achieve your academic goals with confidence.
