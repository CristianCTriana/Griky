Information Literacy

One of the most important skills you will develop as a graduate student is information literacy, which is the ability to find, evaluate, and use information effectively and responsibly. At the graduate level, this skill is not just about locating sources for an assignment; it is about learning how to participate in the scholarly conversations in your field and contribute knowledge of your own.

What Does Information Literacy Look Like in Practice?

In your coursework, you will use information literacy when you: 

  • Search the Trefry Library databases for peer-reviewed journal articles 
  • Decide which sources are most relevant and credible for your research 
  • Integrate evidence into your writing to support your argument 
  • Cite your sources properly to give credit and avoid plagiarism 

Example Scenario

Imagine you are writing a paper on cybersecurity policy. A simple internet search might yield news articles or blog posts, but information literacy pushes you further. You will use the library databases to locate peer-reviewed studies, government reports, and policy briefs. Then you will analyze which of these sources are most authoritative, how they align or differ, and how they inform your argument.

Information Literacy and Your Graduate Journey

Developing strong information literacy skills now will prepare you for larger projects later in your program, such as your capstone or thesis (or dissertation). By learning how to navigate scholarly resources, evaluate evidence, and use information ethically, you are building a foundation that will support your success throughout graduate school and beyond.

Being a scholar means more than completing assignments; it means learning to think critically, question deeply, and contribute meaningfully to your discipline. Information literacy is one of the key skills that enables you to do this.

The Five Steps of Information Literacy

Each step builds on the one before it, helping you move from asking questions to producing scholarly work of your own. 

1. Identify

Define your research question or topic clearly. Ask yourself: What do I really want to know, and why does it matter in my field?

3. Evaluate

Critically assess your sources. Who is the author? Is the publication peer-reviewed? Is the evidence current, valid, and relevant to your question? 

Be mindful of bias. Consider the author's perspective, potential conflicts of interest, and whether the source presents information fairly or with a particular agenda.
