Week 4 Foundations of Scholarship

Reading Scholarly Articles

One thing you should notice immediately when looking at a scholarly article is that it is divided into sections. (Quantitative reports tend to be laid out more formally than qualitative reports.) Usually, the sections of an article are labeled, though the labels may vary slightly from journal to journal. Also, sometimes sections may be combined, depending on the editorial policy of a given journal.

Sections of a Scholarly Article

The following list identifies the typical sections to look for.  

Abstract

The Abstract provides a summary of the entire article. Read it first to get an overview of the study. 

Introduction

The Introduction usually explains why the research was done. Often this section will identify the research question and theoretical framework and then present a short literature review.

Methodology

The Methodology section (sometimes called Methods) explains how the study was conducted and who was being studied.  It typically includes a description of the methods used to collect and analyze the study data. If it's a qualitative report, the researcher may discuss their role in the study.

Results

The Results section may also be called the Findings section. In this section, the author presents a concise report of what the analysis discovered. Tables or figures are often included here to formally display the findings. In a qualitative report, this section will often include descriptions of and quotations from the study’s participants.

Discussion

In the Discussion section, the author explains the significance of the investigation's results in relation to the research question.

Conclusion

In the Conclusion, the author explains what implications the study might have in the greater context. In other words, the author explains what they learned and why it matters. (Sometimes the Discussion and Conclusion sections are presented as a single section.)

References 

The References section provides a list of resources that indicate the other research literature the author consulted as part of their investigation. 

Appendices 

Appendices, if present, can include a range of items: the questionnaire or survey used, more data tables, mathematical proofs or formulas, illustrations, figures, etc.

When we talk about scholarly research, what we are referring to are articles, books, or reports that are based on original research or experimentation. The scholars being referred to are typically scientists, academics, or other experts in a given field who are often connected with a college, university, or similar institution.

[Definition by Professor Timothy C. Lim, California State University.]

You may hear a professor refer to "the literature" when talking about a particular idea or subject area. What he or she is talking about are the articles, books, or other sources that pertain to the research topic under discussion. When you write a literature review, what you're doing is reviewing the literature that addresses your paper's research question or topic.

You've probably also heard the terms quantitative and qualitative in reference to research. Every research report should address specific analytical objectives, types of research questions, data collection methods, the types of data produced, and the degree of flexibility in the design of the study. So you'll want to make sure you also take note of the overall approach in the article you are reading.

Quantitative research is a systematic, empirical investigation that uses statistical, mathematical, or computational techniques. Quantitative research looks to explain the causes of change. The goal is to objectively report. The writing in a quantitative article is typically impersonal and focuses purely on the facts of the study. No mention is made of the person doing the study, the problems that came up, or what the researcher thought about while the study was underway.

Qualitative research is a planned, empirical investigation, the aim of which is to gain a deep understanding of a specific organization, event, or issue, rather than to compile a surface description of a large sample of a population by way of measurement. This approach typically uses unstructured, exploratory techniques. Qualitative research is concerned with understanding what is happening as viewed by the participants. The researcher is often one of those participants. The writing in a qualitative article may use the words "I" or "we" because what is being reported is often a process rather than a finding, a qualitative article may not always follow the section-by-section format. For this reason, reading qualitative reports can be more challenging.

Another important aspect of scholarly research is that it is peer-reviewed. The Merriam-Webster Online Dictionary defines peer review as:

    "The process by which a scholarly work (such as a paper or research proposal) is checked by a group of experts in the same field to make sure it meets the necessary standards before it is published or accepted."

How this works is that an author will submit his or her article to a specific journal. The editor of that journal then relays the article anonymously to several experts in the author's field—that is, his or her peers—who critique the article in terms of its quality and accuracy. They then send their review back to the editor. An article that is favorably reviewed by these experts and the editor is now peer-reviewed and may be published.

We read differently depending on what it is we are reading. Reading a novel or a magazine engages us in a different way than reading an academic article or book chapter. [On the screen is a picture of the book Moby Dick along with a whale graphic.] When we are reading fiction and really enjoying it, we can get lost in the storytelling, and our imagination may be filled with descriptions of the setting and characters.

When reading a scholarly article, the experience is very different. [On the screen is a side-by-side comparison of Moby Dick the book and a journal article on whale watching.] We are reading to understand and to find information. Our approach here needs to be more analytical and critical.

There are different ways of reading: skimming, scanning, extensive reading, and intensive reading. In scholarly research, you are likely to use all of these methods at different times.

Skimming means looking over things very quickly to get a general idea of what the text is about.

Scanning is similar, but here you’re looking for something specific—say, a particular point being discussed.

Extensive reading is reading deeply, often word-for-word. Reading a novel is a kind of extensive reading, but the same approach can apply to a scholarly article you've identified as especially useful.

Intensive reading is more focused—often used to gather specific information, such as when reviewing sources for an annotated bibliography or literature review.

One thing you should notice immediately when looking at a scholarly article is that it is divided into sections. Quantitative reports tend to be laid out more formally than qualitative reports. Usually, the sections are labeled, though labels may vary slightly from journal to journal, and some sections may be combined depending on editorial policy. Here are the typical sections to look for:

  • Abstract: Provides a summary of the entire article.
  • Introduction: Explains why the research was done, identifies the research question and theoretical framework, and presents a short literature review.
  • Methodology (or Methods): Explains how the study was conducted, who was studied, and what methods were used to collect and analyze data. In qualitative reports, the researcher may discuss their role in the study.
  • Results (or Findings): Presents what the analysis discovered, often with tables or figures. Qualitative findings often include participant quotes and descriptive analysis.
  • Discussion: Interprets the results in relation to the research question.
  • Conclusion: Explains the study's implications and relevance. Sometimes combined with the discussion section.
  • References: Lists the research literature consulted.
  • Appendices: May include surveys, additional data, tables, formulas, illustrations, or figures.

If you do a Google search on how to read a scholarly article, you'll find many approaches—but most say the same thing: don’t read linearly like you would a novel.

When reading research, you'll find it more useful to target certain sections first. Your initial goal should be to learn what the article is about at the general level.

Before you begin, make sure you have a clear idea in mind of your topic or research question. Doing this will help you decide if what you're reading is relevant to your research project.

Read the abstract first. An abstract provides a summary of the entire article. Reading it, you can learn about the important concepts of the study without necessarily understanding every detail.

One thing to note here is that all abstracts are not alike. Some are written very well and provide a clear synopsis of the article. Some are written poorly or are incomplete, which means you may have to read the article more intensively. As a rule, however, the abstract is intended to provide a summary. If the information here seems useful, move on to the introduction.

[On the screen: Hint: The abstract is usually  presented right after the title of the article. Sometimes it is located in a sidebar or second page. Articles that have been translated sometimes post the abstract at the end of the document.]

Skim the introduction during this first read-through. Take notes or highlight important text as you do. What you are looking for is a general idea of why the research was done and what the research question is.

Look, too, for special terms that the author might be using. This is important, as sometimes researchers will develop their own jargon to express the concepts they are studying. You'll want to take note of the theoretical framework being used. Sometimes this is explained quite clearly, but sometimes the author may not be as clear as you'd like.

Finally, continue to skim-read through the review of the literature. Your goal here is not to learn about this research in depth, but to understand what the author thought was important in relation to the study.

[On the screen: Hint: Sometimes the literature review in presented in the Introduction.]

Jump over the report of the study’s findings and go straight to the discussion and conclusion sections. This is where the author will summarize what he or she found and how (or if) the research question was answered. This section should show how the study fits into the larger literature and what practical implications the study has, if any.

After reading the discussion and conclusion, you should know what conclusions the researcher drew from the research study and what steps were suggested for future research.

[On the screen: Hint: Recall that sometimes the discussion and conclusion sections are presented separately.]

Once you've read the abstract, introduction, and discussion/conclusion, skim over the rest of the article just to make sure you've read it all.
