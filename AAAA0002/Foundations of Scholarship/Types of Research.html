Week 4 Foundations of Scholarship

Types of Research

Graduate students encounter a variety of research experiences throughout their programs. Some research assignments are designed to help you practice scholarly thinking within a single course. Others are larger projects that build toward the culmination of your degree. Additionally, research may take many forms, ranging from reviewing books and articles in the library to engaging directly with human participants. Understanding these distinctions will help you approach each task with clarity, confidence, and integrity. 

Course Assignment Research vs. Capstone Project Research

Course assignments often involve research projects on a smaller scale. These may include literature reviews, analytical essays, annotated bibliographies, case studies, or even multimedia presentations. The purpose is to strengthen your ability to: 

  • Identify and evaluate credible sources 
  • Apply theories or concepts to real-world situations 
  • Practice academic writing, citation, and scholarly communication 
  • Develop your voice as a graduate-level thinker 

While these assignments may seem self-contained, they are excellent opportunities to test out ideas, refine research strategies, and explore topics you might pursue later in your program.

Course Assignment Example:

In a graduate leadership course, you may be asked to write a 10-page literature review on transformational leadership. Your task would be to locate scholarly articles from the library, summarize the theories, compare approaches, and suggest areas where further research could be useful. This assignment develops your skills in critical reading, synthesis, and academic writing.

  • A thesis or research paper

  • A creative project (in some fields) 

Capstone work is larger in scope and requires demonstrating mastery of your discipline’s methods, theories, and professional standards. Unlike individual course assignments, which emphasize learning specific skills, the capstone challenges you to integrate knowledge across your entire program. It often involves original research or a substantial applied project that contributes in a meaningful way to your field.

Capstone Assignment Example:

As part of your capstone, you might design and conduct an original study on leadership effectiveness in nonprofit organizations. This project could involve gathering data from case studies, surveys, or interviews; analyzing results with appropriate methods; and then writing a substantial research paper that demonstrates how your findings contribute to leadership theory and practice.

Think of your course assignments as a training ground and as your capstone as the proving ground. Together, they prepare you to make the transition from learner to scholar-practitioner.

Library Research vs. People Research

When most students think of “research,” they picture long hours in the library exploring databases, journals, and scholarly books. This type of library research (sometimes called secondary research) is central to academic work. It allows you to:

  • Review and synthesize existing scholarship 
  • Identify gaps in knowledge or debates in the field 
  • Ground your own arguments in credible evidence 
  • Stay informed about current trends, methods, and findings 

Library research strengthens your ability to enter the scholarly conversation, cite sources accurately, and position your work alongside other experts.

Library Research Example:

Suppose you are researching the effects of social media on adolescent well-being. Through library research, you might gather peer-reviewed journal articles, analyze published findings, and then write a paper that synthesizes existing knowledge and suggests areas needing further exploration.

People research, also called human subjects research, involves collecting new data directly from individuals or groups. Data collection methods may include: 

  • Surveys or questionnaires 
  • Interviews or focus groups
  • Observational studies 
  • Experiments or fieldwork 

Because people are involved, this type of research carries additional ethical responsibilities. At APUS, any project that collects data from human participants must follow strict guidelines and often requires approval from the Institutional Review Board (IRB). This process ensures that: 

  • Participants’ rights, privacy, and well-being are protected.
  • Informed consent is obtained.
  • Risks are minimized, and benefits are clearly defined.
  • Data are handled responsibly and confidentially.

People Research Example:

If you wanted to study how veterans experience online learning, you might design a survey or set of interviews with current students. To carry out this project ethically, you would need IRB approval before collecting any information from the research population to ensure participation is voluntary, to protect the participants' identities, and handle data with care.

Important Note

For most course assignments, you will not be asked to conduct people research on your own. Instead, you may be asked to analyze published studies that used human subjects. If your program includes a thesis, dissertation, or applied project that involves collecting your own data from participants, you will work closely with faculty and the IRB to follow all required procedures. 

At APUS, we take the protection of human subjects in research very seriously. Should you not comply with the university's IRB policies, this is considered research misconduct. 

Learn more on the What is an Institutional Review Board (IRB)? [webpage] in the Success Center. 

In short, library research equips you to build knowledge from existing sources, while people research generates new data that can advance the field. Both forms of research are valuable, but they require different skills, tools, and responsibilities.
