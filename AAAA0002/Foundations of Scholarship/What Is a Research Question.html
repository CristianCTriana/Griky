What Is a Research Question?

Your research question is the foundation of your entire research process. Without a strong and well-defined question, your study may lack direction and coherence, putting the success of your research at risk. Watch the following video, What Is a Research Question?, and consider the following questions:

  • What is the definition of a research question, and why is it important in the research process?
  • What are some useful queries to consider when brainstorming and developing a research question?
  • What are some criteria for determining whether a research question is of good quality?


What is a research question? The Writing Center at George Mason University offers a helpful definition:

    “A research question is a clear, focused, complex, and arguable question around which you center your research.”

[Full definition on the screen: “A research question is a clear, focused, concise, complex, and arguable question around which you center your research. You should ask a question about an issue that you are genuinely curious about.”]

A research question is more than a paper topic; it’s a question that ideally captures your interest and curiosity. Like the "X" on a treasure map, the research question focuses and guides the entire research journey.

A topic can be thought of as the subject of a conversation or some situation or event that is thought about. A research question, by contrast, investigates a specific aspect of a topic.

In developing a research question, you want to keep in mind what it is you want to know about a topic. It's a given that you want to know more about it, but what does “more” mean? How do you refine your curiosity into a workable research question? To develop a research question, it helps to brainstorm by asking questions. The Washington University Libraries in St. Louis, MO, suggests these useful queries:

  • How? How does the topic fit with respect to the field?
  • Where? Where does the topic take place, geographically or culturally?
  • When? When did your topic become important or of issue?
  • Who? Who are the people involved or affected?
  • Why? Why is the topic important?
  • What? What makes your research unique? 

These are not the only questions you can ask, but they give you an idea of how you need to be thinking when you explore what it is about a topic that captures your interest. 

Once you've come up with a research question, it is important to determine if it is a good one for you to research. When we say “good,” what is usually meant is whether the question is one that is useful for conducting research. The Department of Psychology at California State University suggests these six criteria for determining the quality of a research question: First, does it answer something new? Second, is the question based on or does it build on previous research? Third, does the question look to the primary work of other researchers rather than secondary work? Fourth, is the research question a specific one? Fifth, is the question simple? And finally, is the research question practical in its feasibility?

We'll take the rest of this video to cover each of these criteria in more detail. 

The purpose of research is to advance knowledge, whether that means we are testing something in a unique way, or testing how well, or if, a theory predicts certain results, or exploring a new research methodology. The point is to develop our understanding of ourselves and our existence. Your research question should serve this goal by aiming to provide some type or degree of new insight.

A research question that is based on an understanding of previous research and theory is much stronger and more relevant to science than a question that is simply inspired by general interest and curiosity. A study grounded in the theory and results of prior investigations is regarded more respectfully by other researchers. A good research question makes itself part of the scholarly conversation.

Another way of making sure that a good research question is based on previous research is to use primary or original research resources in developing the question. Journal articles and scholarly books are considered primary resources as they report on actual investigations conducted by other researchers. In historical research, a primary source is one created at the time of a given event. By contrast, secondary sources describe, summarize, analyze, evaluate, and are derived from or based on primary source materials.

As a side note, papers written for undergraduate classes often rely heavily on secondary sources. This is because the aim of undergraduate education is to introduce students to their respective academic fields. At the graduate level, students are expected to read the primary source research.

When we say a research question should be specific, what is meant is that the question should straightforwardly state what is being asked. The question should be jargon-free and clearly written.

Here's an example of a nonspecific research question: Are dogs smarter than human toddlers? It does include variables—(A) species and (B) intelligence—and it does say something about the direction of the relationship—“smarter than.” But what isn't said is what is meant by “smarter than.” There are many different ways of operationally defining intelligence, one of which should be included in this question. A better research question would be: Do adult dogs score higher than human 2-year-olds in language acquisition after training?

A simple research question isn't a bad question. Keep in mind that you don't have to take on the big questions of your field. It's OK to add just a little piece to the existing research. Keep it simple is a good piece of advice, especially for students new to the research process. You don't want to collect a massive amount of data about multiple variables that you don't know how to interpret at the end. In their enthusiasm, new researchers often take on more than they can realistically handle. It's OK to do a small, focused study.

Along the same lines of keeping things simple, your research question needs to be a practical one. That is, it needs to be something you can complete in the time you have for it. You should ask yourself, "Can I really finish this project by the deadline? Do I have the tools, resources, and sample subjects to answer my question sufficiently?" Be realistic if you can't adjust your question to make things more manageable. Once you've worked out your research question, your project should begin to fall into place.

Check for Quality

Pulled from themes presented in the Duke University guide on What Makes a Good Research Question? [PDF], here are six criteria for determining the quality of a research question: 

  • Is it interesting and debatable?
  • Does it offer a fresh angle or solve a problem?
  • Is the scope manageable—not too broad or narrow?
  • Can I research it with my time and resources? 
  • Do I know what information I need to answer it?
  • Does it connect to a larger issue or context?
