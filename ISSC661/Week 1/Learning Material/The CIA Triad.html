Week 1 Foundations of Information Assurance

The CIA Triad

The central model used for information security is known as the CIA triad (Death, 2023). The CIA triad defines the central objectives of confidentiality, integrity, and availability.

Confidentiality ensures that sensitive information is accessible only to those who are authorized to access it. This is often done through encryption or another access control (Death, 2023). An example of confidentiality would be the need of encryption by a doctor’s office pertaining to patient records that are both stored and data that is transmitted.

Integrity is the principle that ensures data remains accurate and consistent with policy. This is most often done with techniques such as digital signatures or hashing to illustrate that data has not been tampered with (Death, 2023). An example of integrity would be an electronic signature used on loan documents. Before the signature is applied, the system would generate a unique hash of the document. Anything changed after the signature would alter the hash.

Availability guarantees that data and systems are accessible when needed (Death, 2023). This often requires redundancy or a backup and a means of a continuity of operations. An example of availability would be having multiple servers that create backups of data so that in the event of an interruption, access is still available.

Introduction

(0:00) In this lesson we'll look at fundamental concepts that underpin the world of information security. Let's dive in.

(0:07) Information security, often referred to as infosec, is all about safeguarding data resources from unauthorized access, attacks, theft, or damage. Data can be vulnerable at rest, in transit, or during processing. To ensure secure information, we focus on confidentiality, integrity, and availability.

CIA Triad

(0:28) We use the CIA Triad. First, confidentiality: this means that information can only be accessed by authorized individuals. Second, integrity ensures that data remains unaltered and is only modified by authorized parties. Third, availability: this means information must be readily accessible to those authorized to use it.

(0:51) These three principles form the backbone of information security. As security specialists, you'll find you're always seeking the best balance of all three components.

Access Control

(1:02) An access control system ensures that an information system meets the goals of the CIA Triad. Access control dictates how subjects like users, devices, or processes interact with objects like networks, servers, databases, apps, or files.

(1:18) Modern access control is implemented through identity and access management (IAM), consisting of four main processes:

  • Identification: creating unique accounts for users, devices, or processes.
  • Authentication: proving the identity of subjects through credentials.
  • Authorization: determining and enforcing rights for subjects on resources.
  • Accounting: tracking authorized resource usage and detecting unauthorized access.

(1:44) Access control is essential for both people and systems, ensuring that only legitimate actions are permitted.

Non-Repudiation

(1:58) Some security models and researchers identify other properties of secure systems. The most important of these is non-repudiation.

(2:00) Non-repudiation means a person can't deny doing something, such as creating, modifying, or sending a resource. For example, providing proof of authenticity and integrity of data that's sent or received.

Gap Analysis

(2:14) Gap analysis is a process that assesses how well an organization's security systems align with a cybersecurity framework. Security functions have specific goals achieved through security controls.

(2:27) To select the right controls, organizations use frameworks to guide them. Frameworks provide structure and guidance for security programs. Gap analysis identifies deviations from framework requirements and recommends remediation, helping organizations maintain cybersecurity, meet compliance requirements, and prioritize investments.

Zero Trust

(2:48) Zero trust is a security model that treats all devices, users, and services as untrusted, regardless of location within or outside the network's perimeter. This model relies on fundamental concepts for comprehensive security, including adaptive identity, threat scope reduction, and policy-driven access control.

(3:10) Adaptive identity recognizes that identities change and need continuous verification. Threat scope reduction limits access to resources on a need-to-know basis, reducing attack surfaces. Policy-driven access control enforces access restrictions based on user identity, device posture, and network context.

(3:32) Zero trust separates the control plane and data plane for flexibility and scalability. In a zero trust architecture, the control plane manages policies and authorization, while the data plane handles secure data transfer. Separating these planes enhances flexibility and security, allowing for granular control and continuous monitoring.
