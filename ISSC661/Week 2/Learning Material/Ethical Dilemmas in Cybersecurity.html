Ethical Dilemmas in Cybersecurity

Cybersecurity today is not just a technical challenge but a moral responsibility. Korstanje (2023) highlighted how professionals face ethical dilemmas around surveillance, autonomy, and digital inequality, noting that treating personal information as a commodity risks exploitation and manipulation.

These concerns extend into incident response, whereas disclosure and remediation decisions can significantly impact stakeholders, underscoring the need for transparency, accountability, and respect for human dignity.

Similarly, Kumar and Kaur (2023) argued that ethical lapses in e-commerce, such as dark patterns, data harvesting, and algorithmic bias, erode trust and violate user rights.

They stressed that ethics in online platforms must go beyond compliance, advocating for informed consent, accessibility, and inclusivity to ensure vulnerable populations are considered on digital platforms. Both perspectives acknowledge that ethics should be embedded in governance, risk management, and compliance rather than treated as afterthoughts.

The ethical challenges intensify as generative AI advances. Islam (2024) warned that GenAI introduces risks such as deepfakes, misinformation, and automated attacks, requiring cybersecurity to evolve in ways that safeguard privacy and human rights.

He calls for values-based governance frameworks, responsible AI, ethical impact assessments, and human-in-the-loop systems that prioritize fairness, accountability, and transparency (Islam, 2024). GenAI’s reliance on vast datasets raises concerns about consent, data ownership, and re-identification (Islam, 2024).

Although privacy-enhancing technologies (PETs) and differential privacy can mitigate these risks, professionals must still navigate trade-offs between innovation and protection. Echoing Korstanje’s (2023) call for critical reflection and Kumar and Kaur’s (2023) emphasis on inclusivity, Islam (2024) insisted that cybersecurity must be designed with privacy-by-default, stakeholder engagement, and ethical audits to ensure accountability.

Whether in professional practice, e-commerce, or AI-driven systems, ethics must be proactively embedded into digital ecosystems (Korstanje, 2023; Kumar and Kaur, 2023; Islam, 2024).

"By fostering dialogue, transparency, and respect for human dignity, organizations can build more just, inclusive, and resilient environments in an era defined by rapid technological change."
