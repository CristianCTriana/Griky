There are many advantages to implementing cybersecurity governance, risk management, and compliance (GRC). Governance is the framework in which cybersecurity aligns with business objectives, ensuring accountability and oversight in order to manage threats, protect business interests, and maintain operations (Edwards & Weaver, 2024). Governance involves setting policies, assigning roles, and integrating cybersecurity into all decisions being made. On the other hand, risk management is the process of identifying, assessing, and mitigating threats to digital assets. Lastly, compliance ensures that organizations meet legal and regulatory obligations, such as GDPR, HIPAA, and PCI-DSS, to name a few.

Effective cybersecurity governance requires more than technical controls; it demands strategic leadership. Edwards and Weaver (2024) argued that governance must be entrenched in the organizational culture, supported by executive leadership, and have board-level oversight. Continuous monitoring, documentation, and training are needed to maintain adherence. GRC is not a one-time initiative and requires continuous improvement, adaptation, and strategic alignment (Edwards & Weaver, 2024). There are several governance frameworks that provide structured approaches to policy development, risk assessment, and performance measurement (Edwards & Weaver, 2024). Governance also involves ethical considerations, such as transparency, accountability, and respect for user privacy. By aligning cybersecurity with business goals, governance ensures that security investments support long-term resilience and deliver value for the financial outlay. Governance should not be just about control, but about fostering innovation while managing risk. Governance provides the strategic compass that guides cybersecurity efforts across the organization.
