Week 4 Incident Management and Response

The Incident Response Lifecycle

Edwards and Weaver (2024) outlined a comprehensive incident response lifecycle comprising preparation, detection, analysis, containment, eradication, recovery, and post-incident review.

Playbooks and predefined procedures are important, as is testing them regularly (Johansen, 2025). Goldschmidt (2025) added a strategic layer, proposing a capability development model that aligns lifecycle stages with organizational maturity.

Together, these perspectives highlight that effective incident response is both a technical and organizational endeavor.

Key Elements

(0:51) Let's go over the key elements of an incident response plan and important things to consider when you think you may have been the target of a cyber attack.

(1:00) Incident response is typically broken down into six phases:

  • Preparation
  • Identification
  • Containment
  • Eradication
  • Recovery
  • Lessons Learned

(1:11) Most organizations mainly focus on containment, eradication, and recovery—and completely skip lessons learned. But the truth is, this last phase is potentially one of the most important.

Preparation

(1:27) Preparation is as straightforward as making sure you have a trained incident response team—either employed, on retainer, or at least someone's business card so you know who to call.

Identification

(1:42) An incident is initially identified in any number of ways, leading you to start your response plan with only slight awareness of what the incident may be.

(1:51) The identification phase is meant to clear this part up. This phase also includes the investigation of the depth of the compromise, its source, and its success or failure.

(2:01) Identification is done through a review of log files—lots and lots of log files. The important consideration at this point is not to disrupt any potential evidence of the incident.

(2:14) A well-trained and equipped response team will be able to rapidly parse log files, review forensic images, and do so without damaging any evidence in the process.

Containment

(2:28) Containment often happens concurrently with identification or immediately following. Damaged systems are removed from production, devices are isolated, and compromised accounts are locked down.

(2:41) The bleeding stops here.

Eradication

(2:49) Eradication is exactly what it sounds like—removing and remediating any damage discovered in the identification phase.

(2:57) This is normally done by restoring systems from backup and re-imaging workstation systems.

(3:02) It's important to note that proper eradication of a cyber infection should be done by trained professionals and should only be done after comprehensive investigation of the incident is completed.

(3:13) Time and time again, small organizations will be quick to delete, restore, and re-image after the first sign of an incident—before they've learned how the attacker got in or how much damage was really done.

(3:24) In many cases, when systems are reset so quickly, the organization has no way of going back to learn what happened and, as a result, is often hit by the same type of attack again and again.

Recovery

(3:40) Recovery is the testing of the fixes in the eradication phase and the transition back to normal operations.

(3:44) Vulnerabilities are remediated, compromised accounts have passwords changed or removed altogether and replaced with more secure methods of access.

(3:55) Functionality is tested and day-to-day business resumes.

Lessons Learned

(4:04) The last phase is one that many organizations skip, but it's arguably the most important to prevent future incidents.

(4:12) Lessons learned involve reviewing the steps that were taken during each phase and improving both your incident response capability and your security footprint.

(4:21) If you rush to get back up and running but never stop to consider the implications of what caused the security incident, you may never improve your cybersecurity standing.

(4:34) Whether it was human error, security holes, or flaws in a security product, your organization should review what went wrong and use the incident as a stepping stone to work toward a solution.

(4:40) Without this stage, you may find yourself running back through these steps again and again with every subsequent preventable incident.

Preparation involves creating policies, training staff, and establishing communication protocols. An incident response plan (IRP) is the cornerstone of preparedness. IRPs should be aligned with business objectives and regulatory requirements.

Key components include roles and responsibilities, escalation procedures, communication strategies, and integration with business continuity plans (Edwards & Weaver, 2024).

An organization should have a playbook for each incident type (ransomware, insider threats, data breaches, etc.) and should be updated regularly (Johansen, 2025).

An IRP should also evolve through capability assessments and a gap analysis so that organizations assess their current state, desired capabilities, and targeted improvements (Goldschmidt, 2025).

Overall, a fully developed IRP ensures that when an incident occurs, the response is swift, coordinated, and effective.

Detection is the first technical step in incident response and requires monitoring systems and identifying anomalies. This involves identifying signs of compromise through logs, alerts, and behavioral anomalies.

Baselining normal activity is important in order to detect deviations (Johansen, 2025).

The use of Security Information and Event Management (SIEM) systems, intrusion detection systems (IDS), and threat intelligence feeds should be used.

Analysis involves understanding the scope, impact, and root cause of the incident. Digital forensics plays a critical role here through memory analysis, disk imaging, and log correlation (Johansen, 2025).

Analysis is linked to capability maturation, as investing in tools and training enables organizations to grow and overcome incidents more quickly (Goldschmidt, 2025). Ultimately, timely and accurate analysis better informs containment decisions (Edwards & Weaver, 2024).

Containment aims to isolate affected systems and prevent further damage and can be done through network segmentation, account suspension, or service shutdowns (Edwards & Weaver, 2024). However, containment strategies should be based on incident severity and business impact.

On the other hand, eradication involves removing the threat from the environment. This can be done through methods such as malware removal, patching vulnerabilities, and cleaning infected files (Johansen, 2025).

It is critical that the root causes be determined before eradication is carried out to prevent reinfection (Johansen, 2025).

Containment and eradication require coordination across IT, legal, and executive teams (Goldschmidt, 2025). Coordination minimizes downtime and reputational damage.

Recovery restores systems to operational status. Validated backups, system integrity checks, and phased restoration are important (Edwards & Weaver, 2024). Recovery must be documented and monitored to ensure no residual threats remain.

  • Systems must be verified as clean before reintroduction (Johansen, 2025).
  • Recovery is a test of resilience (Goldschmidt, 2025).
  • Post-recovery audits should be performed to identify areas of improvement (Goldschmidt, 2025).

The final stage is reflection through a post-incident review to determine what went well, what failed, and what can be improved (Edwards & Weaver, 2024).

Incident reports should also include timelines, actions taken, and forensic findings to support legal proceedings, insurance claims, and compliance audits (Johansen, 2025).

All of these documents can assist in the feedback loop that encourages incident data to be used for continuous improvements and building long-term resilience (Goldschmidt, 2025).
