Introduction: Cybersecurity and National Security

In today’s digital landscape, cybersecurity is no longer a technical concern; it’s a national security imperative. Cybercrime poses a direct threat to state stability, targeting critical government systems, financial institutions, and communication networks (Ievdokymov, 2024).

The increasing sophistication of cybercriminals, coupled with the anonymity of digital attacks, makes traditional law enforcement mechanisms insufficient. These threats are not isolated; they are:

Transnational Coordinated Politically Motivated

Cybercrime occurs in various forms, such as data breaches, ransomware, phishing, and denial-of-service attacks, to name a few. Each of these can paralyze public services, compromise sensitive data, and erode public trust.

Unfortunately, cybercrime is evolving faster than the legal and technological frameworks designed to combat it (Ievdokymov, 2024). This gap leaves states vulnerable to espionage, sabotage, and manipulation.

The implications for national security are great. A successful cyberattack can disrupt energy grids, manipulate elections, or leak classified information. The interconnectedness of digital systems means that a breach in one sector can cascade across others.

Therefore, cybersecurity must be integrated into national defense strategies, with dedicated agencies, funding, and international cooperation.
