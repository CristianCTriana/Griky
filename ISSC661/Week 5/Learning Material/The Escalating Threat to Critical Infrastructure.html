Week 5 Convergence of Security and Warfare

The Escalating Threat to Critical Infrastructure

Cyberattacks on critical infrastructure (CI) have become increasingly frequent and damaging (Toledano, 2024). CI includes essential systems such as energy grids, water supplies, transportation networks, and healthcare services, to name a few. These systems are attractive targets for cybercriminals and nation-state actors because disrupting them can cause widespread chaos and economic loss.

Critical infrastructure is vulnerable because many systems rely on outdated technology. Legacy software, unpatched vulnerabilities, and poor network segmentation create entry points for attackers (Toledano, 2024).

Additionally, CI often operates in real-time environments, where downtime is unacceptable, making organizations more likely to pay ransoms or comply with attacker demands.

Real-world breaches have demonstrated how a single attack can cripple an entire region. The interconnected nature of CI means that a compromise in one sector, can cascade into others.

For example, ransomware is one of the most destructive methods by encrypting data and demanding a payment for release. The attack on the Colonial Pipeline occurred May 7, 2021 when the group Darkside used ransomware to shut down pipeline activity. To further protect its system, the owners of the pipeline shut down the system and ultimately paid the over $4 million crypto ransom 6 days later in order to restart their business.

Other common attacks on CI include spear phishing, where attackers use personalized emails to trick employees into revealing their credentials or clicking on malicious links. Third party vendors are also a common vulnerability because CI systems often rely on external contractors, software, hardware, etc.

The Target breach occurred in 2013 as a result of a third-party HVAC vendor being compromised in a spear-phishing attack. The attackers used HVAC employees' credentials to gain access to Target's network.

A modernization of CI systems is needed. Multi-factor authentication (MFA) is one defense as it adds a layer of protection against credential theft (Toledano, 2024). Endpoint detection and response (EDR) is another defense, as it monitors devices for suspicious activity. This, along with network segmentation, limits the spread of malware by isolating critical systems. Lastly, regular training and awareness programs can reduce human error that can threaten CI systems.
