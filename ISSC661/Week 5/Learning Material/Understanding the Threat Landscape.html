Week 5 Convergence of Security and Warfare

Understanding the Threat Landscape

Cyber threats can be classified into several domains, each with distinct characteristics and implications. According to Ievdokymov (2024), the following are often used:

1. Unauthorized access

Involves intruders penetrating secure systems to steal or manipulate data.

2. Malware deployment

Includes viruses, worms, and trojans, which can corrupt files, disable networks, or create backdoors for future attacks.

3. Phishing

Often underestimated, remains one of the most effective methods for compromising systems by exploiting human error.

4. Denial-of-service (DoS) attacks

Flood systems with traffic to render them unusable and are particularly disruptive to public services and critical infrastructure.

Misinformation Campaigns

Ievdokymov (2024) also highlighted the role of misinformation campaigns, which use digital platforms to spread false narratives, destabilize societies, and influence public opinion. These attacks can be politically motivated, targeting election systems or government portals during sensitive periods.

The complexity of these threats is compounded by the lack of consistent international laws. Cybercriminals often operate across borders, exploiting jurisdictional gaps and weak enforcement mechanisms. This makes prosecution difficult and prevention critical.

Understanding the threat landscape is the first step toward building resilient systems capable of withstanding and recovering from attacks.
