Week 6 International Standards, Frameworks, and Security Acts

Global Cybersecurity Frameworks

Chourasia et al. (2024) categorize cybersecurity frameworks into three primary types:

Compliance-based frameworks, such as PCI DSS and HIPAA, focus on meeting specific regulatory requirements. 

PCI DSS, while narrow in scope, offers rigorous controls for payment card data.

HIPAA focuses on healthcare data, emphasizing confidentiality and breach notification.

Risk-based frameworks, like NIST CSF and ISO/IEC 27001, prioritize identifying and mitigating risks based on organizational context.

The NIST Cybersecurity Framework (CSF) is widely adopted due to its flexibility and comprehensive structure. It comprises five core functions:

  • Identify
  • Protect
  • Detect
  • Respond
  • Recover

ISO/IEC 27001 is an international standard that emphasizes information security management systems (ISMS) and continuous improvement.

Maturity models, including CMMI and CERT-RMM, assess an organization’s cybersecurity capabilities over time.

Frameworks should be tailored to organizational needs (Chourasia et al., 2024). For example:

Financial Institution

May prioritize compliance with data protection laws.

Tech Startup

Might focus on risk-based approaches.

No single framework is superior as they each serve different needs (Chourasia et al., 2024). These frameworks provide a roadmap for securing infrastructure, training personnel, and responding to incidents.

In addition, they foster a culture of accountability and continuous improvement. Effectiveness depends on organizational context, regulatory environment, and risk appetite.

What is CSF?

(0:55) The cyber security framework has three primary components: the Core, the Tiers, and the Profiles.

Core

(0:59) The Core is a set of cyber security outcomes that can be used to understand: What is it that I need to do in my cyber security program?

(1:24) One of the great things about the Core is that it's written in plain language and gives you an idea of what you need to do.

(1:29) The Core is written in a hierarchical structure, starting out with the Functions at the top, breaking down into Categories, and then into Subcategories at the lowest level.

(1:38) This has been one of the key values of the CSF over the last decade because it allows you to talk to people at the level they are. For example, you can have a conversation with your board at the Function level, talking about how you're protecting your systems and responding to incidents.

(1:52) Then, when you need to talk to your team, you can get into the weeds at the Subcategory level, talking about your backup strategy or the new tool you're going to use for anomaly detection.

(2:01) The Core provides flexibility for companies to figure out what works best for them and talk about it at the level that makes sense—without dictating exactly what you have to do.

Implementation Tiers

(2:12) This leads to the Implementation Tiers, which answer questions like: How should I do this? or How do I make sure I know if it's working?

(2:18) The Tiers can be a great tool for measuring what we've done and telling others about our goals.

(2:21) CSF Tiers characterize the rigor of your cyber security risk governance and management capabilities and are broken down into four levels—from Partial to Adaptive:

  • Tier 1: Partial – Organizations start here with largely ad hoc processes that may not address cyber security risk.
  • Tier 2: Risk-Informed – Indicates a concerted effort to prioritize capabilities and protect what matters most.
  • Tier 3: Repeatable – Organization-wide approach implemented consistently, ensuring everyone meets expectations and senior leadership is informed.
  • Tier 4: Adaptive – Characterized by optimizing capabilities and preparing for future threats before they occur.

(3:04) Many organizations choose to stop at Tier 3. Tier 4 can be expensive but is necessary in some cases.

Profiles

(3:29) The third component is Profiles. A Profile is simply a way to capture what you're doing as it relates to the CSF Core.

(3:37) There are many types of Profiles that could be created, and you don't have to use any of them at all—but they are very helpful.

(3:44) Examples include:

  • Organizational Profiles – Characterize what a specific company is doing.
  • Community Profiles – Characterize outcomes or requirements based on industries, technologies, or threat types.

(3:57) Within organizational profiles, you can have:

  • Current Profile – Defines what your company is doing today.
  • Target Profile – Captures what you think you should be doing in the future.

(4:08) Profiles are flexible in form—they can be bullet points, paragraphs, or even images like Harvey Balls. Do what works for your company and helps you understand and capture what you need to do in the future.

(4:40) The CSF enables easier conversations at every level of an organization—from strategic discussions with your board to technical conversations with your IT and security teams.

Frameworks also differ in their implementation complexity.

ISO/IEC 27001 Requires formal certification.
NIST CSF Allows self-assessment.
Maturity models (CERT-RMM) Provide benchmarking tools but demand extensive documentation.

Organizations must weigh these factors against resource availability and strategic priorities.

Ultimately, the goal is to create a cybersecurity posture that is proactive, resilient, and aligned with business goals.

Frameworks serve as the foundation for this endeavor, guiding policy development, technical controls, and incident response.
