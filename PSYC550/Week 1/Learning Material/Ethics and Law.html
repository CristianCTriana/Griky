Week 1 Introduction to Professional Ethics and Ethical Decision-Making Models

Ethics and Law

Every state has licensing laws and boards to regulate the practice of mental health and enforce state laws. Even though ethics and laws are not the same, many state laws include ethical guidelines.

The state licensing boards identify the minimum criteria for licensure and admittance into the profession, including:

  • National and state exam requirements
  • Graduate education accreditation
  • Hours and type of supervision
  • Internship

The boards establish standards for practice in specialty areas (i.e. school psychology, sexual offender treatment, marriage and family therapy, etc.), licensure renewal criteria, and disciplinary proceedings for violations of the professional conduct standards (Corey et al., 2020).

All ethics codes require compliance with state and federal laws and regulations. Ethics and law conflict in many situations, including informed consent, confidentiality, and advertising.

It is important to remember that in legal situations, laws take precedence over ethical guidelines.

Mental health professionals regularly encounter complex issues that involve professional judgment and ethical quandaries that may have legal implications.

To determine if legal issues are present, Remely and Herlihy (2019) recommend that practitioners consider the involvement of lawyers, status of legal proceedings, and the potential for a complaint to be filed against the practitioner.

It is advised to seek legal guidance from an attorney versus a colleague.
