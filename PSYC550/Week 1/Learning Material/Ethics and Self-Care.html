Week 1 Introduction to Professional Ethics and Ethical Decision-Making Models

Ethics and Self-Care

It is critical that those in helping professions are aware of how personal needs and personality may influence their professional responsibilities. Personal needs may include:

A strong desire to be valued by others The need to be all-knowing or perfect The need to relieve all stress from clients The need to be in an authority position

Working in the helping professions is satisfying and rewarding, while simultaneously stressful and emotionally exhausting. Stress is common in the helping professions. Stress is likely to develop when professionals hold themselves accountable for a client’s progress in or feel pressured to resolve client problems in a quick manner.

Burnout & Signs of Decay

Burnout occurs when one feels depleted mentally, physically, spiritually, and intellectually. It is often accompanied by feelings of hopelessness and helplessness. Corey et al. (2020) suggest that burnout results from a lengthy process of decay. Signs of decay that may lead to burnout include:

  • Poor or no boundaries with clients
  • Preoccupation with wealth and success
  • Practicing above one’s scope of competence
  • Poor nutrition and exercise habits
  • Personal isolation and apathy toward client struggles

When burnout is not managed, physical illness or emotional exhaustion may reach a severity that the impairment may impact the ability to practice within acceptable ethical standards.

Helping professionals have an ethical obligation to maintain their mental stamina and vitality. Maintaining self-care benefits the professional and the client.

Wellness is important to competent practice.
