Week 1 Introduction to Professional Ethics and Ethical Decision-Making Models

Introduction to Ethics

Each mental health professional organization has a code of ethics. The primary purpose of an ethics code is to protect the public by providing guidelines for professional practice. Ethics establish guidelines for professional behavior, but they are not the same as laws.

Unlike laws, ethics are not enforced by the law or government. They are enforced by professional associations (Corey et al., 2020).

The American Psychological Association’s (APA) Code of Ethics (2017) guides the behavior of psychologists. The National Association of School Psychologists (2020) established standards for school psychologists. Professions may also have specialty guidelines. In psychology, these specialty guidelines offer additional considerations for conduct when working with older adults (APA, 2014) or in forensic settings (APA, 2013).

The APA Ethics Code has been revised numerous times since the first publication in the early 1950s to meet the adapting roles and responsibilities of practitioners. The most recent publication was in 2017.

The APA Ethics Code developed from research by Pope and Vetter (1992) in which APA members were asked to identify any ethically challenging incidents they experienced in practice. Additional reading on the study can be found here: Ethical Dilemmas Encountered by Members of the American Psychological Association: A National Survey [website].

The APA Ethics Code (2017) is comprised of the following sections:

  1. Introduction and Applicability
  2. Preamble
  3. General Principles
  4. Ethical Standards
